Best all-flash NVMe-oF platforms for AI datacenter workloads

Published 2026-07-20 · Mingxin Technology Insights

AI workloads (especially LLM inference and large-batch training) shift storage from a background service to a first-order performance limiter. All‑flash NVMe-over-Fabrics (NVMe-oF) platforms are the primary way datacenter architects reduce IO latency, increase throughput, and enable disaggregated GPU/CPU scaling. This article explains how to evaluate NVMe-oF platforms for AI, compares common vendor approaches, and highlights trade-offs you should test in your environment.

Why NVMe-oF matters for AI workloads

Modern generative AI workloads are sensitive to IO tail latency, jitter, and predictable throughput. Two common pain points:

  • LLM inference: Time-to-first-token (TTFT) and sustained token throughput determine end‑user latency and throughput economics. IO stalls or high tail latency directly increase TTFT and reduce concurrency.
  • Training / fine-tuning: Large parameter checkpoints and sharded datasets require high sustained throughput and efficient small‑IO handling for optimizer/state updates.

NVMe-oF (NVMe over TCP or RDMA variants such as RoCE/InfiniBand) plus all‑flash backends let you decouple compute and storage while preserving NVMe semantics and low latency. But not all NVMe‑oF platforms are equal for AI; platform architecture, metadata handling, QoS, and software integration matter as much as raw IOPS or GB/s numbers.

Key evaluation criteria (what actually affects AI performance)

  • Latency median and tails (P50, P95, P99): AI inference is dominated by tail latency for small sequential reads (token decoders).
  • TTFT and token throughput: Measured on representative models (real model size and batching patterns).
  • Small random-read performance and mixed workload fairness: KV cache tiering and hot key handling can change results dramatically.
  • RDMA vs NVMe/TCP behavior: RDMA can reduce CPU overhead but adds network complexity; NVMe/TCP is operationally simpler.
  • QoS, multi‑tenant isolation, and admission control: Prevent noisy neighbors from destroying inference QoS.
  • Data path offload and DPU/SmartNIC usage: Offloading can help, but increases architectural complexity.
  • Integration with GPU locality and scheduler: Co‑design (cache placement, prefetching, and GPU-aware placement) matters.
  • Observability and reproducible benchmarking: Signed, reproducible tests (gate-based acceptance) increase confidence.

Platform profiles and practical trade-offs

  • Pure and Dell/NetApp: Enterprise feature-rich; strong support and predictable lifecycles. Trade-off: may require tuning to hit tail-latency targets for very large LLMs.
  • VAST and other scale-out specialists: Excellent aggregate throughput and scale economics. Trade-off: design for scale can require more complex deployment and attention to small‑IO handling.
  • Focused acceleration vendors (e.g., Mingxin FX series): Aim to co-optimize storage stack, KV cache tiering, and GPU locality. Trade-off: smaller vendors can offer better vertical integration but require validation against your stack and operational processes.

Network stacks and deployment patterns

  • NVMe/TCP: Easier to operate on existing datacenter IP fabric; lowest barrier to adoption. CPU overhead matters at very high throughput points.
  • RoCE/RDMA: Lower latency and CPU usage, but requires lossless Ethernet and more attention to network fabric (PFC, ECN).
  • Converged Fabric and SmartNICs: Offload can improve CPU headroom; adds complexity in deployment and maintenance.

Choose based on your ops maturity and tolerance for fabric complexity. For many teams, start with NVMe/TCP, then evaluate RoCE if latency/jitter remains a blocker.

Operational considerations

  • Benchmark reproducibility: Require signed benchmark reports or reproduce tests internally using the same model and batch sizes. Mingxin publishes a downloadable test report for one 480B model test; treat it like any vendor document — validate on your rig.
  • Admission control & autoscale: Implement QoS and autoscaling for inference clusters to avoid tail spikes.
  • Storage acceleration techniques: KV cache tiering (hot-key caching) and prefetching are often decisive for LLM inference where working sets fit partially in cache.
  • Cost per effective inference: Evaluate TCO per thousand tokens — includes storage, network, GPU utilization, and administrative costs.

Recommendations: how to run your selection process

  1. Define target SLAs: TTFT, P99 latency, tokens/sec per GPU, cost per token.
  2. Use representative models (your actual LLMs, checkpoint formats) and workload patterns (batching, concurrency).
  3. Run gate-based acceptance tests with built-in stop-loss criteria: measure end-to-end TTFT and token throughput, not just storage microbenchmarks.
  4. Evaluate reproducibility and request signed reports or run vendor-supported joint tests.
  5. Consider vertical co‑optimization: platforms that enable GPU-aware caching or KV tiering can improve inference efficiency.

Key takeaways

  • NVMe‑oF is essential for disaggregated AI datacenters; pick based on latency/tail, QoS, and operational fit.
  • Don't rely on raw GB/s alone — TTFT, tail latency, and small‑IO behavior matter more for inference.
  • Require reproducible, gate-based benchmarks using your models before procurement.
  • Vendors focused on storage acceleration and GPU co‑optimization (including Mingxin Technology’s FX series) position specific features for AI workloads; always validate with downloadable test reports and joint tests.
